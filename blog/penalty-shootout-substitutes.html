The Hook

The 119th-Minute Substitution

It's a move we see more, not less. In an analytics era chasing the highest-percentage option, managers increasingly send on a fresh, specialist taker in the dying minutes, purely for the shootout.

On 29 June 2026 the Netherlands did exactly that, then watched substitute Justin Kluivert hit the post and go out of the World Cup. England fans remember Euro 2020: Rashford and Sancho, on in the 119th minute, both missed.

There's something cruel about it. The player barely touches the game, then the whole tournament rests on his one kick.

So does the gamble work? I built a dataset of 840 shootout kicks from 88 men's international shootouts (World Cup, Euros, Copa América; 1990–2026) to find out.

The Baseline

How Often Does a Shootout Penalty Go In?

First, the yardstick. Across all 840 kicks, the overall conversion rate is 72.0% (95% CI 68.9%–75.0%).

Roughly three in four shootout penalties are scored. Missing is the exception, which is exactly why a missed sub stands out.

For scale: even Lionel Messi has converted about 78% of his career penalties (114 of 147, shootouts aside), not far above this baseline. The spot-kick humbles almost everyone.

The Finding

It's the Specialist Taker Who Pays

It's not substitutes in general who falter. Across all subs, the gap (67.3% vs 72.8% for starters) is only borderline.

The effect sits with the specialist takers, sent on at 105'+ largely to take a kick. They convert just 57.1%, and the most extreme, on at 110'+, only 50.0%.

Since the hypothesis is directional, subs predicted to do worse, the one-sided test is the relevant one; the two-sided figure is shown too.

A logistic regression agrees: being a substitute multiplies the odds of scoring by 0.77 (95% CI 0.55–1.06, p = 0.108). A substitute's scoring odds are roughly 23% lower than a starter's. For comparison, published tournament studies put substitutes near 61% against 74% for starters.

By Minute of Entry

Fresh Legs Help, Until the Last Gasp

Fresh legs aren't the problem. Substitutes who come on with time to settle hold up fine, including those sent on early in extra time (90–104'). Only at 105'+, with barely a touch before the kick, does the line drop below the 72.8% starter mark (dashed).

The fresh-legs gamble looks worst exactly where managers reach for it most: the last-gasp specialist, sent on at 105'+ to take a kick.
The Specialists

The Specialist Takers, One by One

The closest cases to the hypothesis: every taker sent on at 105'+, often with the shootout in mind. As a group they scored 20 of 35 (57.1%).

Yet these are, on the whole, good penalty takers. In open play, the 29 with a career record convert 88% (459/523) — several with flawless records (Sarabia, Sancho, Willian) still missed here. That gap, 88% in normal play versus 57.1% in the shootout, is the point.

A Caveat

Why This Isn't a Clean Experiment

A substitute is not a random starter. Part of the gap may reflect player quality, not just cold legs.

But it often cuts the other way. The late sub is frequently a designated specialist, sent on precisely because he is one of the squad's best takers, with a strong record in open play.

That even these specialists miss more often suggests the conditions (a long wait, then one cold, decisive kick) matter more than the player. Either way this is observational data: the pattern is real, but it does not prove the substitution itself is the cause.

Scope

This analysis covers shootout penalties only. In-play penalties, taken in open play rather than a shootout, convert a touch higher (around 75% historically) and carry different pressures. Everything here is shootout-specific.

Methodology & Sources

840 shootout kicks from 88 men's international shootouts (FIFA World Cup, UEFA Euros, Copa América), 1990–2026. Each taker is classified as a starter or a substitute, with the substitute's minute of entry, by joining the shootout result to the match lineups.

Two sources. StatsBomb open data (event-level data with exact substitution minutes) is the ground truth for recent tournaments. Wikipedia knockout-stage pages, which carry both the shootout box and full lineups with substitution minutes, provide the historical coverage. The Wikipedia parser was validated against StatsBomb on the 18 shootouts present in both sources, matching 100% on outcome sequences and substitute counts.

Confidence intervals use the Wilson method; group comparisons use a two-proportion z-test (one- and two-sided) supported by a logistic regression. Lineups for the live 2026 fixtures were taken from published match reports; a handful of older Copa América matches still lacked lineup data and are counted in the baseline but excluded from the starter-vs-substitute split. The 2026 World Cup is ongoing, so figures are a snapshot.
